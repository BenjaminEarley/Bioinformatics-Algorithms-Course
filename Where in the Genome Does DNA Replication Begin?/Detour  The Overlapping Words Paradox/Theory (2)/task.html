Given two k-mers A and B, the correlation of A and B, denoted Corr(AB) = (c0,…,ck−1) is a k-letter binary word such that ci = 1 if B (k − i)-overlaps with A, and 0 otherwise. The correlation of the above strings is 010011. The correlation frequency of A and B is defined as

\( K_{A,B} = c_0 + c_1 \cdot \dfrac{1}{2} + c_2 \cdot \left(\dfrac{1}{2}\right)^2 + \cdots + c_{k-1} \cdot \left(\dfrac{1}{2}\right)^{k-1} \)


For the strings A and B in the above figure, their correlation is "010011" and their correlation frequency is

\( K_{A,B} = (1/2) + (1/2)^4 + (1/2)^5 = 19/32 \).


Mathematician John Conway suggested the following deceivingly simple formula to compute the odds that B will defeat A:

\( \left.\left(K_{A,A} - K_{A,B}\right)\middle/\left(K_{B,B} - K_{B,A}\right)\right. \).

Conway's proof of this formula was never published, and Martin Gardner, a leading popular mathematics writer, said the following about the formula:

I have no idea why it works. It just cranks out the answer as if by magic, like so many of Conway's other algorithms.
